## 1. Brand identity at a glance  *(Confidence: High)*

**Best In Brands (BIB)** is the authorized distributor of international consumer brands to the Bangladeshi market, with 25 years of operating history, 400+ employees, a portfolio of 120+ brands, and offices in Bangladesh, Singapore, and Dubai. Core categories span fragrances, makeup, skincare, home, toys, food & beverage, and sport, with prestige beauty as the lead category in outbound communications.

**Vision:** *Be the leading authorized distributor in Bangladesh through strong and sustainable partnerships with global brands.*

**Mission:** *To connect Bangladeshi consumers to international brands by constantly diversifying and curating authentic products.*

**Values (stated):** Diversity, Respect, Authenticity, Empowerment, Ethicality, Team Spirit.

**Tagline:** None. BIB does not currently use a tagline or campaign slogan. *(See Open Questions.)*

## 2. Audience  *(Confidence: High)*

The guidelines in this document are calibrated for **B2B communications**: the primary reader is a prospective or current brand partner, international manufacturer, retail channel, or investor evaluating BIB as their Bangladesh market gateway. A secondary audience is internal leadership and board.

BIB's writing should assume a trade-literate reader. Industry shorthand (SOB, YOY, prestige tier, omni-channel) is used without definition, and market data — macroeconomic, retail, and consumer-behavior — is treated as the common language of the conversation.

Consumer-facing copy (for end shoppers) is **out of scope** for these guidelines. It almost certainly lives under the Sundora brand, which appears to operate with its own identity; flag any consumer-facing request and escalate before writing.

Market segmentation referenced in source: "Middle & Affluent Class" (MAC), "mass to affluent" income tiers, "value-driven and curious consumers." These are descriptors of the end market, not of the reader.

## 3. Voice attributes  *(Confidence: Medium-High)*

BIB's voice is **confident, pragmatic-aspirational, data-forward, and formally neutral**. It earns trust through evidence density — years of operation, brand count, channel footprint, market statistics — rather than through stylistic flourish or emotional appeal. Copy is declarative and economical: noun phrases carry the weight, adjectives are used sparingly, and humor, colloquialisms, and campaign-style language are absent.

Four attributes define the voice:

**Authoritative.** BIB writes from the position of a market insider who has earned the right to speak for the Bangladesh opportunity. Claims are backed with numbers ("25 years of expertise | 400+ employees | 120+ brands"), cited sources (HSBC forecasts, market research), or named channel partners (Unimart, Qrius, Artisan, Daraz). Assertions without evidence should be avoided.

**Precise.** Word choice is deliberate and legally careful. BIB says "authorized distributor" — not "partner," not "agent." It says "consumers" — never "customers" or "clients." It says "curating" — not "stocking" or "selling." These choices signal a company that takes its role seriously and knows its category vocabulary.

**Opportunity-oriented.** The tone is grounded in facts but oriented toward growth. Paragraphs tend to move from market reality to market potential. The emotional register is measured optimism, not hype.

**Reserved.** BIB does not perform warmth, playfulness, or luxury drama, even when the portfolio includes Tom Ford, YSL, and Prada. The brand uses "prestige," not "luxury." It describes products as "long-lasting" and "authentic," not "transformative" or "indulgent." Restraint is the register.

### We Are / We Are Not

| **We are…** | **We are not…** |
|---|---|
| Confident and evidence-led | Boastful or self-congratulatory |
| Precise with category and legal terminology | Loose, casual, or interchangeable with synonyms |
| Data-forward (numbers, sources, named partners) | Anecdotal, feeling-led, or abstract |
| Pragmatic and grounded in market reality | Aspirational in a disconnected or campaign-y way |
| Opportunity-oriented | Hype-driven or promissory |
| Formal and neutral in register | Colloquial, playful, or humorous |
| Understated (using "prestige") | Overstated (using "luxury," "ultimate," "transformative") |
| Focused on the partnership and the market | Focused on the product as hero |
| Respectful of the reader's industry literacy | Over-explaining basic trade terminology |
| Authentic and specific about what we distribute | Vague about legal status ("partner," "agent," "rep") |

## 5. Messaging architecture  *(Confidence: Medium-High)*

BIB does not publish a formal messaging framework, but three pillars emerge consistently from the source material. Use these to structure outbound communications.

**Pillar 1 — Market access & expertise.** BIB is the shortest path for a global brand into Bangladesh. Substantiate with: 25 years of operating history, 400+ employees, 120+ brands, a tri-country office footprint (Bangladesh, Singapore, Dubai), and named retail channel relationships (Unimart, Qrius, Artisan, Daraz). Frame Bangladesh's scale using the HSBC forecast that the country will be the world's 9th largest consumer market by 2030.

**Pillar 2 — Authenticity.** In a market where fake and clone imports are a named threat, BIB's role as an *authorized* distributor is both a brand value and a functional differentiator. Use the word "authentic" when it is earned by context — e.g., the authorization chain, the brand portfolio, the prestige positioning — and avoid using it as a generic filler.

**Pillar 3 — Omni-channel retail capability.** BIB's proprietary retail arm (Sundora) and distribution across travel retail, e-commerce, chain stores, and flagship formats is the operational proof that a brand partner's product will actually reach consumers. This pillar translates category credentials into distribution certainty.

**Credentials block (reusable).**

> 25 years of expertise · 400+ employees · 120+ brands · Offices in Bangladesh, Singapore, Dubai

Use as-is in brand summaries, LinkedIn bios, email signatures, and deck footers.

## 6. Terminology & lexicon  *(Confidence: High)*

### Preferred terms

| Term | Use for | Don't replace with |
|---|---|---|
| consumers | End-market shoppers | customers, clients, users, shoppers |
| authorized distributor | BIB's role | partner, agent, reseller, rep |
| brands | Product lines and the companies behind them | manufacturers, suppliers, vendors |
| curating | What BIB does with its portfolio | stocking, carrying, selling |
| connecting | BIB's role between brand and market | introducing, selling to, delivering to |
| prestige | The premium beauty tier | luxury, high-end, upscale |
| value-for-money | The mass tier | cheap, budget, affordable |
| long-lasting | Functional benefit (fragrance, makeup) | durable, lasting, staying |
| authentic | Genuine, authorized provenance | real, legitimate, original |
| omni-channel retail | Sundora's format | multi-channel, cross-channel |
| Middle & Affluent Class (MAC) | The primary consumer segment | middle class, upper middle |
| SOB (share of business), YOY (year-over-year) | Trade shorthand, fine unabbreviated too | — |

### Avoid

Avoid **luxury** (BIB consistently says "prestige"). Avoid **sell / selling / sales** as framing verbs in outbound copy; BIB prefers *connecting*, *curating*, and *distributing*. Avoid **cheap / budget** even when referring to mass-tier products; the frame is *value-for-money*. Avoid **partner** as a synonym for BIB's own role (it's a distributor — "partner" is what it has *with* brands). Avoid campaign-style adjective stacks ("ultimate," "transformative," "revolutionary," "iconic"); the register is reserved.

### Signature phrases

- "connect[ing] Bangladeshi consumers to international brands"
- "authentic international brands" / "authentic products"
- "strong and sustainable partnerships"
- "constantly diversifying and curating"

Reuse these in mission language, about-us copy, and partner-facing communications. They are the closest thing BIB has to a brand lexicon, and recurrence is what makes them recognizable.

## 7. Writing patterns  *(Confidence: Medium)*

**Sentence shape.** Prefer short, declarative sentences and noun-phrase-heavy blocks. When a full paragraph is needed (about-us copy, market analysis, consumer behavior notes), sentences stay medium-length, neutral in register, and front-load the fact before any interpretation.

**Paragraph structure.** Lead with the concrete (a number, a named partner, a dated milestone), then the implication. Reverse this order only when the implication is explicitly the news.

**Headlines.** Headline-equivalents in the source are short, often all-caps section labels (e.g., "INTRODUCING OUR COMPANY"). For long-form, prefer noun-phrase headlines over sentence headlines, and avoid question headlines.

**Evidence density.** Every outbound asset should carry at least one anchoring proof point — a number, a date, a cited source, a named brand, or a named retail partner. Copy without proof points tends to drift out of voice.

**What not to do.** Don't anthropomorphize the brand ("BIB believes," "BIB dreams," "BIB is passionate about"). Don't open with rhetorical questions. Don't use exclamation points. Don't stack more than two adjectives on a noun. Don't use "we" to perform warmth — use it to make commitments.

## 9. Quality checklist

Before publishing any BIB-voiced content, verify:

- [ ] The piece names a specific number, date, cited source, or named partner somewhere in the first third.
- [ ] Every claim about BIB's scale, history, or portfolio is traceable to a source of record.
- [ ] "Authorized distributor" is used where BIB's role is named; no synonym substitution.
- [ ] "Consumers" is used for end-market shoppers; "customers/clients" has not crept in.
- [ ] "Prestige" is used for the premium tier; "luxury" has not crept in.
- [ ] No more than two adjectives stacked on any noun.
- [ ] No exclamation points. No rhetorical question headlines. No anthropomorphizing ("BIB believes…").
- [ ] If the piece is consumer-facing or Sundora-branded, it has been escalated — not written against these guidelines.
